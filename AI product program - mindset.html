The program in one arc

What the full curriculum adds up to when translated into product and design language.

Phase 1
Start with behavior, not hype
Main idea
The opening modules frame AI as a design problem before a technology choice. The first question is what intelligence the system needs to exhibit, what scope it covers, and how success gets measured. Covers M1–M3.
Why it matters
This stops teams from beginning with a fashionable model and forcing it into a problem. The frame prevents the most common — and most expensive — AI failure mode.
Phase 2
Learn the technical stack well enough to make better decisions
Main idea
ML, deep learning, transformer, and GAN modules are decision tools for product teams: what kind of problem fits which method, where data is the bottleneck, when complexity is justified. Covers M4–M5.
Why it matters
Teams make stronger choices when they know when simple prediction is enough, when generation needs grounding, and when the model is not the hardest part.
Phase 3
Design the interaction layer and the human system around the model
Main idea
HCI and Superminds modules extend AI work beyond output quality — into interface design, trust, user control, team cognition, and the role of computers within larger human decision systems. Covers M6–M7.
Why it matters
Even an accurate model fails if the interface is unclear, the automation level is wrong, or the human role is poorly designed. The interaction layer is the product.
Phase 4
Move from pilot experiments to credible business value
Main idea
The final modules ground AI work in people, data, leadership support, KPIs, deployment, and long-term maintenance. It reframes AI product work as an operational system, not a lab demo. Covers M8–M9.
Why it matters
The model is never the full source of advantage. Workflow redesign, governance, UX, data pipelines, and maintenance are what turn raw capability into durable product value.

Six program-wide takeaways

The strongest reusable lessons across all nine modules.

Strategy
Frame AI through outcomes, scope, and data reality
Start by identifying the target behavior, the business outcome, the KPI, the scope of the problem, and the data required. This is more reliable than starting with a preferred model or vendor.
Execution
Complementary assets create real value
The model itself is rarely the full source of advantage. Workflow redesign, domain expertise, governance, UX, and maintenance are what turn raw model capability into usable product value.
GenAI
Generative AI must usually be grounded
LLMs are versatile, but context limits, hallucination risk, and variability mean that retrieval, structure, tools, and review are often required for dependable product use.
UX / HCI
Interface quality shapes trust as much as model quality
Good AI experiences make uncertainty legible, preserve user control, reduce effort, and support correction. Poor AI UX destroys confidence even when the model is competent.
Human + AI
Think people + computers, not people vs. computers
AI works best when it strengthens group memory, synthesis, option generation, and decision quality inside a broader human system — not when it automates away human judgment.
Strategy
Performance benchmarks evolve fast — design for iteration
Benchmark performance shifts quickly. Product and platform teams should design for iteration rather than assume a static ceiling on AI capability.

Module walkthrough

Each module translated into practical product and design language.

01
AI Product Design Foundations
AI categories, four design stages, value creation, scope, performance targets
StrategyFoundationsFraming

What it teaches

  • AI product design begins by defining the behavior the system should achieve, not by choosing a model first.
  • Four design activities: desired behavior, business process fit, data and model choices, and development planning.
  • AI value often comes from pairing the model with complementary business-process changes, not from the model alone.

Why it matters

  • Design: Define the exact user-facing behavior before discussing model class, vendor, or architecture.
  • Product: "Make the workflow smarter" is a much weaker goal than "reduce triage time by 30% while maintaining accuracy."
  • Execution: Gives a shared language for discussing AI ideas across PM, Engineering, and Leadership.
"AI product work is part problem framing, part system design, and part business design. It should never be reduced to 'add AI here.'"
02–03
ML Algorithms & Deep Learning
Supervised, unsupervised, classification, regression, CNNs, RNNs, training intuition
Model fitEvaluationArchitecture

What it teaches

  • Match the method to the problem: regression for continuous outputs, classification for labels, clustering for hidden structure.
  • ML lifecycle discipline: train/validate/test partitioning, overfitting, bias-variance tradeoffs, hyperparameter tuning.
  • Deep learning extends ML through multilayer networks. CNNs suit spatial data; RNNs suit sequences. Complexity only justifies when it improves real generalization.

Why it matters

  • Design: Many business problems do not require deep learning. A simpler model may be better if it solves the workflow clearly.
  • Product: Strong demo performance on familiar data means little if the model fails on production variation.
  • Execution: Algorithm choice is a promise about explainability — decision trees give receipts; neural nets give verdicts.
"Use deep learning when pattern complexity requires it, not as a prestige default. The simplest model that solves the problem is usually the right model."
Yijun's Project
Trained classifiers on real datasets and applied rigorous train/validate/test discipline. Compared decision tree versus neural network on the same problem — the simpler model was often sufficient and always more defensible in a cross-functional review.
04
Designing AI for Superhuman Performance
Superhuman targets, data augmentation, ethics, consent, privacy, crowdsourcing
High-stakes AIData strategyEthics

What it teaches

  • Frame AI design around a precisely defined performance target — including "superhuman" benchmarks measured against real expert performance.
  • Scope specificity matters: what exact condition, decision, or stage is the system responsible for?
  • Introduces data augmentation for small samples, transfer learning, IRB approvals, informed consent, and crowdsourced annotation with fairness guardrails.

Why it matters

  • Design: In high-stakes domains, performance requirements should be defined against real operational risk, not benchmark curiosity.
  • Product: The sharper the target behavior, the more coherent the data strategy and evaluation plan become.
  • Execution: Ethics, consent, and privacy are design constraints from day one — not legal clean-up at the end.
"The most important design work happened before any code. Defining 'better than a radiologist at early-stage motor decline' is a product brief — with a user, a metric, and a use case. The brief precedes and shapes the model."
Yijun's Project — Alzheimer's Detection Classifier
Built ML classifier for early Alzheimer's detection using motor function data. Applied ResNet with augmentation for small clinical sample constraints. Designed crowdsourced annotation workflow with fairness guardrails. Analysed IRB requirements and informed consent as quality-improving constraints — not bureaucratic obstacles.
05
Generative AI & Transformers
Tokenisation, embeddings, attention, RAG, chain-of-thought, tool use, agents
GenAILLMsPrompt design

What it teaches

  • Transformers process language through tokenisation, embeddings, contextual encoding via attention, and probabilistic generation.
  • Prompting alone is insufficient for most real product tasks. RAG, chain-of-thought, tool use, and agent orchestration each address specific limitations.
  • Hallucination is a product risk that shapes UX, content boundaries, and review patterns — not just a model bug.

Why it matters

  • Design: Treat GenAI as a system architecture choice — real products need prompt + retrieval + tools + verification + fallback.
  • Product: The strongest use cases are constrained: grounded Q&A, structured drafting, summarisation with review — not open-ended chat.
  • Execution: Reliability comes from orchestration around the model, not from the model alone.
"Prompt engineering is information architecture. You are structuring what the model attends to, in what sequence, with what constraints. The IA migrated from navigation menus to natural language. Same discipline. Higher stakes."
Yijun's Project — Health Consultation AI
Designed prompts for a sensitive medical consultation use case (gynaecological health). Experimented with constraint framing, tone calibration, and specificity levels. Built RAG pipeline for domain-specific grounding. Designed explicitly for the hallucination failure state: when the model is confidently wrong, does the user have a recovery path?
06
Designing Intelligent Human-Computer Interfaces
HCI foundations, devices, modalities, applications, adaptive interfaces, trust
UX / HCIInteraction designTrust

What it teaches

  • Three HCI areas: building new devices, defining interaction techniques and modalities, and designing applications for specific users and contexts.
  • AI can improve personalisation, prediction, and assistance — but also fails visibly in speech, automation, and action-taking systems.
  • Critical question: how much AI involvement are people comfortable with when the system moves from sensing to acting?

Why it matters

  • Design: AI UX must be evaluated at the interaction level. Users experience the system through clarity, control, and feedback — not model accuracy.
  • Product: Good AI interaction makes uncertainty understandable, helps users recover from errors, and avoids over-automation.
  • Execution: Interface design is part of intelligence design. Trust, effort, and legibility are product requirements.
"Who is the user of an alert? The AI generates one signal. Three stakeholders have three incompatible needs. Deciding who gets what, in what form, at what threshold — that is the design work. Not the algorithm."
Yijun's Project — Silent Risk Moments (Elderly Care)
Designed a multi-stakeholder AI monitoring system for falls, medication errors, dehydration, and cardiac anomalies. Built three alert architectures: patient-facing (dignity-preserving), family-facing (contextual, non-alarming), clinical-facing (protocol-formatted, prioritised by urgency). Resolved the three-user notification trilemma through role-based information design.
07
Superminds: Org Design as AI Design
People + computers, hyperconnectivity, five cognitive processes, five supermind types
Human + AIDecision systemsCollaboration

What it teaches

  • The key shift: from "humans in the loop" to "computers in the group." AI is part of a collective intelligence system.
  • Five cognitive processes all intelligent entities share: create possibilities, decide, sense the world, remember, and learn.
  • Five supermind types — hierarchies, democracies, markets, communities, ecosystems — each optimising for different cognitive bottlenecks.

Why it matters

  • Design: AI opportunities often sit in team cognition: better synthesis, memory, option generation, prioritisation, forecasting.
  • Product: Designing for group workflow can be more valuable than a single-user tool when decisions are collective and cross-functional.
  • Execution: Every product feature is a silent organisational redesign. That shift should be explicit — not accidental.
"When you add a recommendation engine, you shift cognitive work from user judgment to machine prediction. That shift should be named, deliberated, and owned. When it happens by accident, automation erodes agency without solving the underlying problem."
Yijun's Project — Supermind Org Design
Designed organisational processes leveraging superminds for collective decision-making. Applied prediction market logic to strategic risk — discovering aggregated forecasts significantly outperformed individual experts. Analysed a community supermind failure caused by mismatching structure to problem type.
08
GANs, Synthetic Media & Ethical Frontiers
Generative adversarial networks, deepfakes, sustainability, feasibility, responsibility
GANsMedia AIEthics

What it teaches

  • GANs use adversarial training between a generator (creates synthetic data) and a discriminator (classifies real vs. fake) to reach a high-quality equilibrium.
  • Applications: super-resolution, style transfer, deepfakes, historical restoration, medical data synthesis.
  • Three-pillar ethics framework: sustainability (long-term viability), feasibility (technical + economic), responsibility (societal impact + accountability).

Why it matters

  • Design: GANs are the canonical dual-use technology. The same capability that restores archival photos produces undetectable political deepfakes.
  • Product: Governance gap is a design failure, not a policy problem. Ethics framework applied before deployment — not after harm.
  • Execution: Disclosure architecture and authentication protocols matter more than detection accuracy in the arms race against synthetic media.
"The designer's job is not to solve the trolley problem. It's to build systems that make the scenario less likely — and to be rigorously transparent about how the system behaves when it can't be avoided."
Yijun's Project — Moral Machine & Media Ethics
Designed AV ethical policy via the Moral Machine interface. Evaluated TecoGAN for archival restoration. Assessed Huru.ai interview coaching for effectiveness and potential demographic bias. Analysed the deepfake detection-vs-generation arms race — conclusion: disclosure architecture matters more than detection accuracy.
09
Business Case & Scale
Problem identification, double diamond, Christensen's disruption theory, ROI, maintenance
ExecutionStrategyBusiness case

What it teaches

  • Double Diamond for AI: Discover → Define → Develop → Deliver. Each phase has specific deliverables and failure modes.
  • Business case requires baseline quantification, gap sizing, full cost modelling (including maintenance — which frequently exceeds development), and realistic timelines.
  • Christensen's disruption taxonomy: sustaining innovation makes existing things better; disruptive innovation changes who the customer is.

Why it matters

  • Design: Missing subject matter expert on the team predicts failure more reliably than any technical constraint.
  • Product: AI ROI rarely materialises in under 12 months. Business cases that stop at launch are systematically undercosted.
  • Execution: Model decay is not a bug — it is the physics of data-driven systems. Design for retraining, monitoring, and deprecation from day one.
"Most AI features I've shipped are sustaining innovations — making existing things marginally better for existing users. True disruptive AI changes who the customer is. That is a different brief, team structure, and success metric than most design orgs are built to pursue."
Yijun's Project — Disruption Analysis & Business Case
Applied Christensen's disruption taxonomy to the generative AI landscape — mapping existential vs. incremental pressure on incumbents. Built business case with 18-month cost model. Analysed manufacturing AI implementation failures to reverse-engineer what successful cases require and what organisational conditions they assume.

Practical playbook

Reusable patterns and checklists derived from the program.

AI Opportunity Framing

  1. Describe the user or business problem in plain language.
  2. State the desired system behavior precisely.
  3. Define the KPI and the user-visible impact.
  4. List what data exists, what is missing, and what is expensive to obtain.
  5. Choose the method family only after the above is clear: predictive, classificatory, generative, agentic.
  6. Define "done" before architecture discussions begin.

Before You Build — 8 Questions

  • Is the problem precisely defined with measurable success criteria?
  • Is there sufficient, representative, high-quality data available?
  • Is domain expertise (not just technical expertise) on the team?
  • Have ethics, consent, privacy, and accountability been addressed?
  • Does the AI enhance human judgment — not replace it inappropriately?
  • Is there a rigorous ROI model including 18-month maintenance cost?
  • Does the team and org structure match the intelligence type needed?
  • Is there a monitoring, retraining, and deprecation plan?

HCI Design Principles for AI

  • Make uncertainty legible. Users should know when the AI is guessing.
  • Preserve user agency. The user should always have a path to override, correct, or escalate.
  • Design the handoff explicitly. Where does machine confidence give way to human judgment? That line is a design decision.
  • Error recovery first. Design the failure state before the success state.
  • Transparency is a feature. Disclose when AI is involved in decisions that affect users.

Ethics Framework (Three Pillars)

  • Sustainability. Is this viable long-term without causing harm? Consider environmental impact, societal effects, and long-term trust.
  • Feasibility. Is it technically and economically practical? Assess maintenance burden, data availability, and team capability.
  • Responsibility. What is the societal impact and who is accountable when things go wrong? Consent, privacy, and disclosure are non-negotiable.
Apply before deployment. Ethics retrofitted post-launch costs orders of magnitude more than ethics designed in from the start.

Supermind Selection Guide

  • Execution at scale → Hierarchy with AI coordination tools.
  • Strategic forecasting → Market supermind + prediction aggregation.
  • Diverse option generation → Community or ecosystem with AI synthesis.
  • Democratic decisions → Democracy supermind with AI facilitation.
  • Long-term innovation → Ecosystem design with internal + external contributors.
Key question: Which cognitive process is the bottleneck — perception, judgment, prediction, planning, or coordination? Match the structure to the gap.

GenAI Product Patterns

  • For accuracy: RAG — ground responses in retrieved facts, not model memory.
  • For complex reasoning: Chain-of-thought — ask the model to reason step by step.
  • For multi-step tasks: Agentic systems — define goal, plan, tools, and reflection loop.
  • For reliability: Add verification layers, structured output, and human review checkpoints.
  • For trust: Always design for the hallucination failure state — what happens when the LLM is confidently wrong?
